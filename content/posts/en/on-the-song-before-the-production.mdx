There's a question I find myself returning to whenever I sit down to arrange something, or whenever I try to explain why a particular cover moves me more than the original. The question is: *what does this song sound like before anyone decides what it sounds like?*

Strip away the snare reverb, the guitar tone, the synth pad that sits underneath the second chorus, the side-chained bass, the choice to put a third harmony above the lead in bar 17. What you have left is the skeleton — melody, lyric, chord motion, phrasing. That skeleton is the song. Everything else is a costume.

## Why this matters to me

It's an old idea — songwriters have been saying *if it works on an acoustic guitar at the kitchen table, it works* for as long as there have been kitchen tables — but it's only recently that I figured out why I take it personally.

The reason is that the bare version is the closest we get to **the composer's actual intention**. The production is the costume, and costumes are negotiated with collaborators, scheduled with deadlines, fitted to genres and audiences. The bare song is what the writer brought home from wherever the song came from. It's emotionally honest in a way the produced version sometimes can't be — not because production is dishonest, but because it has more goals to serve.

This is why I like hearing a writer's own demos, why I like *Unplugged* records, why I like covers that strip a song back to its bones. The bare version answers a question the produced version often doesn't: *what is this actually about, harmonically and melodically?*

## What this means for arrangement work

When I arrange something, the first thing I want to know is what the skeleton looks like. Sometimes that's obvious — the song has a clear melody, four chords, a single emotional direction. Sometimes the skeleton is partially obscured by the production it was born in, and the work is partly archaeological: which moves are the song, which moves are the record?

An arrangement that doesn't understand this difference tends to fail in a specific way. It feels busy. It adds layers because layers are the texture of "produced music," not because each layer is responding to something in the skeleton. You end up with five things going on, none of which is the song.

The arrangements I'm proudest of are the ones where I could remove any single layer and the song would still mean what it means — just thinner. That's the test. If a layer is load-bearing, the skeleton wasn't strong enough.

## Why I'm writing about this here

Because this site is partly a place to write down the principles I keep almost-articulating in conversations and then losing. This is one of them. I'll come back to it from other angles — there's a related point to make about how this maps onto guitar pedal stacking, of all things, and another about why Italian *cantautorato* tends to survive translation badly and acoustic stripping beautifully.

But I wanted the first principle on the page before I start using it as a foundation.
